The Struggle for Sovereignty

A significant historical trend was the so called 'struggle for sovereignty' in the the 19th century in New Zealand. Prior to the 19th century, Europeans and the native Māori had had a chequered past, from a miunderstanding between explorer Abel Tasman's crew and Māori to the successful nature of trade. Over the 19th century, forces such as the Treaty of Waitangi to the New Zealand wars would prove to affect both the Māori and the Pakeha people.

The Treaty of Waitangi

A highly significant force that affected the peace of New Zealand was the signing of what many perceive to be New Zealand's founding document, the Treaty of Waitangi.

Why was the Treaty signed?

The Treaty of Waitangi was created in order to facilitate an agreement between the Māori and the English. The English wished to lay claim to New Zealand in order to stop oncoming French claimants and create law and order to protect the newly arriving settlers organised by the New Zealand Company in England, whcih was headed by Edward Gibbon Wakefield.

Problems with the Treaty

Translation issues in the hastily comprised Treaty meant that English and Māori were left having two differing ideas about what the Treaty actually meant. For example, in the First Article where Māori were to cede their sovereignty, sovereignty was incorrectly translated to kawanatanga, rather than rangatiratanga. Kawanatanga roughly translated to 'governorship' in Māori. This meant that Māori thought they would be protected by the actions of the Governor, Hobson. However the British believed they had complete control over Māori governance.

Additionally, the British right of preemption was translated as hokonga. Māori interpreted this as the first right of refusal. They believed that they could offer their land to the British, but then sell it on afterwards. However to the British the right of preemption meant that the Māori could only sell to the British exclusively.

The role of the governors in the 1840's

Following the annexation of New Zealand, the Colonial Office in Britain exercised authority by proxy through a Governor. In reality this authority was at first restricted to isolated spheres of Pakeha influence, mainly in the north of the North Island. These areas were known as beach-heads.

The Wasteland Issue

New Zealand's first Governor, William Hobson, was instructed by the Colonial Office to assume control of wastleland for the Crown. The Crown interpreted 'wasteland' to mean all land that was not cultivated or occupied. For Māori there was no such thing as wasteland. All land was turangawaewae and had a purpose. A later Governor, Governor Grey, who was known as the 'hitman of colonialisation' used the poicy of wasteland to seize 30 million acres of the South Island, and 3 million acres of the North Island. This allowed Pakeha authority to move beyond the beachheads, and move into the interior of New Zealand.

Investigation of pre-1840 land purchases

There was considerable Māori and Pakeha concern over the Governor's investigation of pre-1840 land claims. Simple claims up to 2560 acres were allowed if the Land Claims Commissioner determined that the land had been fairly purchased. For claims exceeding this amount, the excess was deemed surplus land and became the property of the Crown.

Māori subject to English law

In addition to this, Māori became subject to English law. A example of this was in the case of a murderer called Maketu. In accordance with English law, he was hanged for his crimes. In Māori culture, he would have most likely been banished instead. This incident made Māori question the respect of their customs by settlers.

The creation of the Settler Government

The British settlers who arrived in New Zealand after its annexation brought with them the expectation of self-government following a British pattern. Accordingly they began to lobby the Governor and the Colonial Office in Britain for a constitution that would allow them the limited self-government enjoyed in other colonies.

The 1852 Constitution Act

Ultimately a constitution was written in 1846 and presented to the Colonial Office for consideration. An amended form of the 1846 Constitution was eventually passed in 1852, once the Māori and Pakeha populations were eventually at numerical balance. The 1852 Constitution Act was significant for Māori-Pakeha relations because:

  • Māori lost the protection of the Governor and for many this had been a major reason for signing the Treaty of Waitangi.
  • The franchise was based on an individual title for land. In practice this excluded Māori who did not have Pakeha-style written titles for property and who 'owned' land communually from a proper share in the exercise of power in New Zealand.

Result of the creation of the settler government

The above factors ensured a settler government whose concern was for settler interests. Māori were effectively excluded from the machinery of the Pakeha state.

The formation of the Kingitanga

As Māori uneasiness with Pakeha threats to their authority grew, Māori began to seek a different way to assert their sovereignty, through the creation of the Kingitanga movement.

How was it created?

In the early 1850's a series of tribal runanga were held to discuss kotahitanga (self-government). From them emerged the desire for pupuri whenua (retention of land). It was decided that this could be achieved by placing the mana of all tribes under the mana of a King. Additionally, the King's responsibilites would extend to providing law and order as many Māori had complained that the Governor had failed to do so.

The great Waikato chief Te Wherowhero was crowned King Potatau I in 1858, and was succeeded by his son Tawhiao. A large section of the North Island looked to the King as their authority figure, excluding the Nga Puhi.

What were the effects of the creation of the Kingitanga?

The creation of the Kingitanga essentially created separate zones of Māori and Pakeha authority. Neither the settlers nor the Government were pleased. Governor Gore-Browne felt threatened by this direct challenge to British sovereignty, and many settlers regarded the Kingitanga as a land league that would impede their acquisition of land.

In 1860 Gore-Browne called the Kohimarama conference attended by 200 chiefs. Gore-Browne hoped to divide Māori loyalties by gaining the chiefs' condemnation of the Kingitanga. Instead those Māori present at Kohimarama showed that they shared a common understanding that the Treaty guaranteed their mana. They viewed the Kingitanga as a legitimate expression of Māori authority guaranteed by the Treaty.

The meeting produced the Kohimarama Covenant in which signatories pledged to do nothing inconsistent with their declared recognition of the Queen's sovereignty, essentially undermining the Kingite movement. For Māori, the Queen's representatives in New Zealand had nominal sovereignty, whereas the Government believed theiur substantive sovereignty had been reaffirmed.

The New Zealand Land Wars 1860-72

One way in which Māori and Pakeha competed for sovereignty was through warfare. The wars of the 1840's had been limited and localised. They were concluded in a way that did not compromise the mana of either side. In contrast, the wars of the 1860-72 period entailed pan-tribal involvement and ultimately led the Pakeha to resort to law to assert their sovereignty.

War in Taranaki 1860-61

The immediate cause of the Taranaki War was a dispute over the Waitara Block - a piece of land which a local of Te Ati Awa had sold without the consent of the paramount chief Wiremu Kingi. Governor Gore-Browne's insistence that the Waitara Block be alienated contrary to Kingi's wishes, was a response to the popular Pakeha opinion that to comply with Kingi would be to recognise his authority. Kingi was not opposed to Pakeha presence in New Zealand, but he was opposed to land sales that contridicted his mana.

The British wished to achieve a decisive victory by engaging the Māori in a large scale battle, where their greater numbers and artillery would be superior. However, they did not anticipate the Kingitanga's support of Kingi, nor did they anticpate the Māori military prowess, including the development of the modern pa.

After a year of fighting the British had failed in their objective of a swift victory. The Kingitanga had won the allegiance of most Taranaki tribes. The British failure to assert sovereignty in Taranaki led to the attempt to crush the Kingitanga in its own territorial and economic base; the Waikato.

The War in the Waikato 1861-1863

In 1861 Governor Grey was reinstated as Governor in November, and as historian Claudia Orange points out: 'began a war on two fronts.'

On the political front he set up runanga: consultative assemblies intended to undermine the Kingitanga. This was unsuccessful, as well as his attempts to win over Kingitanga. These were unsuccessful attempts to flatter the chiefs and give them gifts to win them over.

However it was clear that Grey was preparing for war, as he had the Great South Road built as a military supply line from Auckland to the Waikato River. His reports to the Colonial Office deliberately exaggerated the threat of Māori to Auckland, prompting the Imperial government to send troops to New Zealand.

Lieutenant Duncan Cameron led the Pakeha force into the Waikato. They were vastly superior in numbers and resources to the Māori, allowing for a continuous offense. In contrast, the Māori numbered 4000 troops to the 14000 Pakeha, and had to employ a rotational system where not all of these troops were in the field at once.

After defending unsuccessfully at Meremere and Rangiriri, although not allowing the British a decisive victory, the King's capital Ngaruawahia was captured in December 1863. The Māori retreated to Orakau where they fought valiantly but were defeated. The Kingitanga now only existed beyond the limits of Ngati Maniapoto territory, now known as the King Country. A boundary was set up known as the aukati line, and marked where Pakeha territory ended and Māori control began.

The Battle of Gate Pa

The focus now became the Bay of Plenty, and General Cameron intended to wipe out the Kingitanga's allies and support. He attacked Gate Pa and suffered a heavy defeat. A few days later at the Battle of Te Ranga he avenged that defeat when sixty Māori where caught working on new fortifications.

This battle was seen by historians as the end of concentrated military resistance by the Kingitanga. No decisive victory had been won by the British, but the overall effect did much damage to the Kingitanga. The Kingitanga's resources and land were severely depleted and their economy shattered.

Pakeha assertions of sovereignty through law in the 1860's

It is now commonly accepted that the major cause of Pakeha success in the struggle for sovereignty was the Pakeha use of law.

Unlike the wars, the laws affected all Māori. They brought all Māori into the orbit of the Pakeha political system and served to marginalise economically. In her book 'The Treaty of Waitangi'(1987) Claudia Orange calls the laws 'the most serious attack on the vitality of Māori life.'

The New Zealand Settlement Act, 1863

This law enacted the confiscation of about 4.73 million acres of the North Island. Although the raupatu as Māori knew it, was supposed to take land from 'rebel' tribes, it was also applied to the lands of some neutral and kupapa tribes. About half of the confiscated land was later returned. However, the interventing period had caused some Māori communities to experience major social and economic dislocation, resulting in permanent alienation from their land.

The Native Lands Act, 1865

This Act established the Native Land Court which had a profound effect on Māori communities. The courts began the process of converting traditional Māori multiple ownership to individual European style titles.

In 1865 blocks of land owned by multiple owners were required to have no more than 10 owners listed on the title. Both the courts and Māori claimants to a title became tied up in lengthy court processes.
